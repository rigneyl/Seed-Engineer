Author Comparator

Notes for a writing tool that lets a writer compare their prose against a curated set of authors, and expand the comparison arsenal with new profiles.

Concept Notes

Core Idea

A writing tool that allows a writer to compare their writing against a set of landmark authors. The tool analyzes surface-level style traits and helps the writer see which author orbit they are drifting toward. It also lets the user expand the writer comparison arsenal and add new authors over time.

Included Authors

Gabriel Garcia Marquez · Virginia Woolf · Ernest Hemingway · Toni Morrison · Haruki Murakami · Charles Bukowski · Jane Austen · Franz Kafka · Jorge Luis Borges · Maya Angelou · William Faulkner · Edith Wharton · Yukio Mishima · Fyodor Dostoevsky · Truman Capote

Author Style Notes

Gabriel Garcia Marquez

Gabriel Garcia Marquez, known for his distinctive writing style, masterfully employs magical realism to blend the ordinary with the fantastical. His prose is characterized by rich imagery, lyrical language, and a deep sense of nostalgia. Through vivid descriptions and poetic metaphors, Marquez creates a dreamlike atmosphere that transports readers to imaginative realms.

“The world was so recent that many things lacked names, and in order to indicate them, it was necessary to point.”

This sentence exemplifies Marquez’s ability to merge the mystical and the everyday, infusing his storytelling with enchantment and evoking a sense of wonder.

Virginia Woolf

Virginia Woolf’s writing style is characterized by her innovative use of stream-of-consciousness narration, which delves into the inner thoughts, emotions, and perceptions of her characters. She seamlessly weaves together past and present, exploring the complexities of human consciousness. Her prose is marked by its poetic quality, introspection, and attention to detail.

“What a lark! What a plunge! For so it always seemed to her when, with a little squeak of the hinges, which she could hear now, she burst open the French windows and plunged at Bourton into the open air.”

This sentence showcases Woolf’s ability to capture a character’s immediate sensory experiences and their profound impact on their inner world within a single fluid sentence.

Ernest Hemingway

Ernest Hemingway’s distinctive writing style is known for its simplicity, brevity, and understated power. He employs short, concise sentences and minimalistic descriptions, relying on dialogue and action to convey meaning. Hemingway’s prose often contains an iceberg effect, where much is left unsaid, inviting readers to actively engage and interpret the subtext. His writing evokes a sense of realism and immediacy, immersing readers in the raw emotions and experiences of his characters.

“The world breaks everyone and afterward many are strong at the broken places.”

This sentence captures Hemingway’s ability to convey profound truths with economy of language, leaving an indelible impact on the reader.

Toni Morrison

Toni Morrison’s writing style is poetic, evocative, and deeply rooted in the African American experience. Her prose flows with lyrical beauty, while her vivid imagery and powerful symbolism bring her narratives to life. Morrison explores themes of race, identity, and the haunting legacies of history with profound insight. Her sentences are meticulously crafted, carrying emotional weight and capturing the complexities of human existence.

“She is a friend of my mind. She gather me, man. The pieces I am, she gather them and give them back to me in all the right order.”

This sentence demonstrates Morrison’s ability to weave language, emotions, and the human experience into a tapestry of profound beauty.

Haruki Murakami

Haruki Murakami’s writing style is characterized by a blend of magical realism, surrealism, and mystery. His narratives often feature ordinary characters thrust into strange and dreamlike situations, creating an atmosphere of ambiguity and introspection. Murakami’s prose is understated yet evocative, with a focus on introspective internal monologues and a sense of detachment from reality.

“I have this strange feeling that I’m not myself anymore. It’s hard to put into words, but I guess it’s like I was fast asleep, and someone came, disassembled me, and hurriedly put me back together again.”

This sentence showcases Murakami’s ability to convey a sense of existential dislocation and contemplation.

Charles Bukowski

Charles Bukowski’s writing style is raw, gritty, and unapologetically honest. Known for his semi-autobiographical works, Bukowski’s prose often delves into the darker sides of human existence, addressing themes of alcoholism, poverty, and societal alienation. His writing is marked by straightforward and stripped-down language, devoid of flowery embellishments. With an unfiltered approach, Bukowski captures the struggles and contradictions of ordinary people.

“I never met another man I’d rather be. And even if that’s a delusion, it’s a lucky one.”

This sentence reflects Bukowski’s characteristic blend of self-awareness, resignation, and a touch of irony.

Jane Austen

Jane Austen’s writing style is characterized by wit, irony, and social commentary. She masterfully captures the intricacies of Georgian-era society and the lives of women within it. Her prose is precise and elegant, often infused with subtle humour and keen observations of human behaviour. Austen’s sentences are carefully constructed, conveying depth of character and subtle nuances.

“Vanity and pride are different things, though the words are often used synonymously. A person may be proud without being vain.”

This sentence exemplifies Austen’s ability to use concise language to explore complex themes and highlight societal conventions and individual traits.

Franz Kafka

Franz Kafka’s writing style is often described as Kafkaesque, characterized by its surreal, nightmarish atmosphere and exploration of existential themes. He employs precise and concise prose that delves into the complexities of the human psyche and the individual’s struggle against oppressive systems. Kafka’s narratives often feature absurd situations, bureaucratic entanglements, and a pervasive sense of alienation and guilt.

“It was as if the shame of it should outlive him.”

This sentence captures Kafka’s ability to convey a profound sense of existential dread and the burden of guilt.

Jorge Luis Borges

Jorge Luis Borges is known for his distinctive writing style that blends elements of fantasy, philosophy, and intricate storytelling. His works often challenge traditional narrative structures and blur the line between reality and fiction. Borges employs intricate, labyrinthine plots, rich symbolism, and intellectual puzzles to explore themes of time, identity, and the nature of literature itself. His prose is concise, thought-provoking, and filled with vivid imagery.

“The universe (which others call the Library) is composed of an indefinite, perhaps infinite, number of hexagonal galleries.”

This sentence captures Borges’ ability to create vast and enigmatic worlds through his evocative and precise language.

Maya Angelou

Maya Angelou’s writing style is characterized by its lyrical beauty, emotional depth, and powerful storytelling. She weaves together poetry and prose, capturing the struggles and triumphs of marginalized communities, particularly African Americans, with profound grace and resilience. Angelou’s words flow rhythmically, immersing readers in her personal experiences and inviting them to empathize and reflect. Through vivid imagery and evocative language, she addresses themes of identity, discrimination, and the human spirit’s capacity for resilience.

“You may not control all the events that happen to you, but you can decide not to be reduced by them.”

This quote showcases Angelou’s ability to inspire and uplift readers with her empowering and resilient voice.

William Faulkner

William Faulkner’s writing style is characterized by its intricate narrative techniques, experimental structure, and stream-of-consciousness storytelling. He often employs multiple narrators, non-linear timelines, and richly descriptive prose to explore the complexities of Southern culture and the human condition. Faulkner’s sentences are often long and complex, weaving together thoughts, memories, and emotions to create a vivid and immersive reading experience.

“My mother is a fish.”

This brief yet haunting sentence encapsulates Faulkner’s ability to convey profound ideas with startling simplicity and lyrical potency within the larger tapestry of his intricate narratives.

Edith Wharton

Edith Wharton’s writing style is characterized by its elegant prose, social criticism, and keen observation of upper-class society. She excels in portraying the complexities of human relationships, particularly within the confines of societal expectations and the struggle for personal freedom. Wharton’s attention to detail and precise language create a vivid and immersive reading experience.

“Each time you happen to me all over again.”

This sentence showcases Wharton’s ability to convey deep emotions and capture the nuances of love and longing within the constraints of societal conventions.

Yukio Mishima

Yukio Mishima’s writing style is precise, poetic, and deeply rooted in Japanese tradition. He crafts lush descriptions that evoke vivid imagery and explores themes of beauty, death, and the clash between tradition and modernity. Mishima’s prose reflects meticulous attention to detail, conveying profound introspection and a sense of emotional intensity.

“The air of the temple was laden with the fragrance of flowers, and the graves, laid out on the mountain slopes like gardens of stone, lay quietly under the haze.”

This sentence exemplifies Mishima’s ability to create evocative scenes through his rich, sensory language.

Fyodor Dostoevsky

Fyodor Dostoevsky’s writing style is characterized by its psychological depth and exploration of the human psyche. He delves into complex moral and philosophical dilemmas, presenting characters with conflicting desires and existential struggles. Dostoevsky’s prose is introspective, often examining the darker aspects of human nature. His sentences are meticulously crafted, conveying a sense of urgency and intensity.

“Above all, don’t lie to yourself. The man who lies to himself and listens to his own lie comes to a point that he cannot distinguish the truth within him.”

This sentence exemplifies Dostoevsky’s ability to delve into the depths of human consciousness and portray internal conflicts with piercing clarity.

Truman Capote

Truman Capote’s writing style is characterized by its meticulous attention to detail, evocative descriptions, and narrative voice that blends journalism and literary finesse. He masterfully combines factual reporting with storytelling techniques, creating a captivating and immersive reading experience. Capote’s ability to capture the essence of his subjects and infuse his prose with vivid imagery is evident in his work.

“The village of Holcomb stands on the high wheat plains of western Kansas, a lonesome area that other Kansans call ‘out there.’”

This sentence showcases Capote’s talent for setting the scene and drawing readers into the heart of his narratives.
